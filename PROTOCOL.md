# Research protocol

## Question

Where, when, and how do pain-care systems fail patients, and which observations
support or weaken that hypothesis across Australia, the United Kingdom, and the
United States?

“Incompetence” is not a coded outcome. Record observable events: delay, refusal,
abrupt change, missing explanation, lack of follow-up, contradictory instructions,
administrative blockage, preventable harm, or guideline-discordant care.

## Evidence classes

- **PATIENT_REPORT:** attributed pseudonymous account; not independently verified.
- **RECORD:** clinical or administrative record reviewed with identifiers removed.
- **GUIDELINE:** current primary-source professional or government guidance.
- **LAW:** enacted law or binding decision identified by jurisdiction and date.
- **ANALYSIS:** interpretation that cannot promote itself into another class.
- **OUTCOME:** dated change in pain, function, safety, access, or treatment.
- **UNVERIFIED:** claim lacking adequate support.

## Required method

1. Collect consent and keep the consent record outside Git.
2. Assign a random participant identifier; never encode initials or dates.
3. Separate events from interpretations and diagnoses from reported symptoms.
4. Record baseline before associating an outcome with a treatment change.
5. Use pain intensity alongside function, sleep, mood, adverse effects, and goals.
6. Preserve evidence against the research hypothesis.
7. Verify jurisdictional claims against current primary sources.
8. Require clinical review for medical interpretation and qualified legal review
   before describing conduct as unlawful, negligent, or professional misconduct.

## No causal overclaim

A temporal sequence is not proof of causation. Case reports may identify patterns
and falsifiable questions; they cannot estimate prevalence or compare nations
without representative sampling, denominators, consistent definitions, and
confounder control.
