# PATIENT-001 source-claim audit

The source is an AI-generated clinical/legal brief. Its persuasive tone is not
evidence. The raw document is excluded from this public repository.

| Claim from source | Initial status | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Abrupt opioid discontinuation can cause serious harm in a physically dependent patient. | SUPPORTED GENERALLY | TGA, CDC, HHS, and NICE warn against abrupt discontinuation in most non-emergency situations. |
| Any taper must be individualized and monitored. | SUPPORTED GENERALLY | Primary guidance emphasizes individual assessment, monitoring, and adjustment. |
| A patient must consent before any taper can occur. | OVERSTATED | Shared decision-making is emphasized, but NICE expressly addresses cases where agreement cannot be reached and a prescriber believes continuation is not in the person's best interests. |
| Severe symptoms always require restoration of the previous dose. | UNVERIFIED/OVERSTATED | Guidance supports pausing, slowing, or modifying many tapers; “always” and a mandated dose restoration require exact source support and clinical context. |
| The described person legally qualifies for an Australian “exceptional circumstances” exemption. | UNVERIFIED LEGAL/CLINICAL CONCLUSION | A document cannot determine individual eligibility without records, prescriber assessment, and applicable prescribing/PBS rules. |
| Non-consensual tapering is necessarily unlawful or professional misconduct. | UNSUPPORTED AS STATED | Guidelines and rights frameworks are not themselves a case-specific adjudication. Emergency risk, capacity, clinical judgment, state law, and facts matter. |
| A specific rare multi-condition phenotype makes opioid continuation medically necessary. | UNVERIFIED | Requires records and specialist assessment; diagnosis does not by itself establish a particular prescription. |
| The law and guidelines are “entirely on the patient's side.” | REJECTED AS RESEARCH LANGUAGE | Adversarial certainty obscures uncertainty and conflicts among duties, evidence, and clinical judgment. |

## Safety correction

The source should not be used as a prescription demand, complaint of proven
misconduct, or instruction to alter medication. It can be used to formulate
questions for clinicians and qualified advocates.
